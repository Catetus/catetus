# SplatBench gets a second real outdoor scene — `stump` from Mip-NeRF 360

**Status:** draft (2026-05-15). Follow-up to the v0.1.2 release blog.
**Audience:** anyone who clicked through the v0.1.2 leaderboard, asked "are those numbers on a real scene?", and wanted a longer answer than "yes, bonsai and bicycle."

---

The v0.1.2 release blog headlined a **3.78× compression-ratio lead vs `@playcanvas/splat-transform` v2.1.1**. That number was honest, but it was a median across the 13-scene synthetic corpus + bonsai (Mip-NeRF 360 indoor). The single real outdoor scene we had — `bicycle` — was in the leaderboard's main report but **not in the third-party encoder bench**, so the headline gap didn't include a real outdoor data point against splat-transform.

This post fixes that. We pulled a second real outdoor scene (Mip-NeRF 360 `stump`, 944 MB, 3.81 M splats) into SplatBench, ran the full pipeline against it, and ran splat-transform on the same file. The point isn't to invent new numbers; it's to extend the existing bench to a category — **large real-outdoor 3DGS** — that the synthetic proxies couldn't fully represent.

## Why not Sweet Corals?

The original target was the canonical **Sweet Corals** outdoor-coral photogrammetry tile referenced as `sc-tile2` in our private research notes (2.8 M splats, used in PostHAC + SwVQ + DifferentiableRepack validation). That asset lives on a private Blurry Drive — there is no public canonical URL, and SplatBench is a public corpus that cannot depend on internal credentials.

We considered `garden` and `flowers` from Mip-NeRF 360 next. The HuggingFace `dylanebert/3dgs` mirror only carries those as `.splat` files (PlayCanvas format), not the Inria PLY layout used by the rest of the SplatBench corpus. **Mip-NeRF 360 `stump` at iteration 7,000** is the closest available real outdoor 3DGS scene with a public Inria-PLY mirror. It complements `bicycle` (organic foliage + tree-stump texture vs bicycle-on-grass) and gives the leaderboard a second large real-outdoor anchor.

Substitution is documented in `benches/scenes/real/manifest.json` and reproduced by `benches/scenes/scripts/pull-sweet-corals.sh` (script name kept for traceability against the private research queue's references to "Sweet Corals").

## Numbers

Source PLY: **944,270,460 bytes / 3,807,536 splats / SH degree 3**, `blake3:316a4c1c5fac8fc6070dc20b8a4709a513057b684be3a31c192aedebc8cb683c`.

| Tool / preset | Output | Bytes | Ratio vs PLY |
| --- | --- | ---: | ---: |
| `catetus optimize --preset web-mobile` → SPZ | `.spz` | 41,108,592 | **22.97×** |
| `catetus optimize --preset size-min` → SPZ | `.spz` | 29,328,581 | **32.20×** |
| `catetus optimize --preset web-mobile --compress` (glTF buffer + zstd) | `.bin.zst` | 52,954,062 | 17.83× |
| `@playcanvas/splat-transform@2.1.1 --morton-order` | `.sog` | 51,312,100 | **18.40×** |

The Catetus `web-mobile` SPZ vs splat-transform SOG gap on the real outdoor scene is **1.25×** (22.97 / 18.40). That's **substantially smaller than the synthetic median gap of 3.78×** (21.88× Catetus median / 5.79× splat-transform median on the synthetic + bonsai corpus from the v0.1.2 leaderboard). Two honest reads:

1. **The synthetic corpus over-states our lead.** Synthetic scenes generated by `benches/synth_scenes.py` have stronger spatial coherence than real photogrammetry — they're random-but-Gaussian-distributed splats over smooth shells, which our quantization steps eat extremely well. Real outdoor capture has texture detail at every scale that compresses less neatly, and `splat-transform` (whose SOG path is well-tuned to real captures) closes most of the gap.
2. **We're still ahead on real outdoor, and ahead by more than enough to matter.** 1.25× on a real 944 MB scene is the difference between a 51 MB SOG and a 41 MB SPZ delivered to a Safari user on cellular — and that 10 MB delta is *before* the fidelity report, the deterministic-by-version guarantee, the hosted API, the PR badge, and everything else the v0.1.2 release blog talks about. The compression lead is a tiebreaker; the production surface is the product.

`splat-transform` on stump took **928.7 s (~15m29s) wall, peak 1.56 GB RSS** (k-means dominated at 14m28s) on the same M-series laptop where `catetus optimize --preset web-mobile` ran in **2.1 s**. Latency and peak memory are the third and fourth axes the synthetic corpus didn't surface either — a **~440× wall-time gap** on a real 944 MB scene matters for any pipeline that wants to optimize a backlog of captures or run in CI on every PR.

Fidelity (ΔE94 / SSIM / pixelmatch) is not reported on this scene. The in-process Playwright WebGL viewer used by `catetus fidelity` cannot load a ~100 MB optimized glTF within its 60 s `captureFrames` timeout in the public-CI sandbox — the same constraint that produces `bicycle`'s "borderline" labels. The fidelity column will light up once the desktop-class WebGPU fidelity harness lands.

**Biggest licensing risk we flagged:** the *original* target — Sweet Corals — has no public license terms at all because the asset has no public release. Building a public benchmark on a private Drive link would mean either (a) every external contributor needs Blurry's permission to reproduce, or (b) we'd be redistributing without a license. Substituting to a CC-BY-4.0 Mip-NeRF 360 scene kills that risk entirely; the trade-off is that the "real-coral-photogrammetry" texture profile is now under-represented in the public corpus until a license-clean coral scene materializes.

## Reproducing

```bash
bash benches/scenes/scripts/pull-sweet-corals.sh
./target/release/catetus analyze --pretty benches/scenes/real/stump_iter7000.ply
./target/release/catetus optimize --preset web-mobile \
    -o .tmp/stump-runs/web-mobile.gltf \
    benches/scenes/real/stump_iter7000.ply
./target/release/catetus convert --to spz \
    -o .tmp/stump-runs/web-mobile.spz .tmp/stump-runs/web-mobile.gltf
SCENES=stump_mipnerf360_iter7k node benches/run-encoders.mjs
```

`catetus analyze` runs in ~3.9 s on M-series; `catetus optimize` runs in ~2.1 s per preset.
